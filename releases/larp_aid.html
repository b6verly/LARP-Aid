Motor vehicle collision

🛡️
Scene safety & size-upPark uphill/upwind, offset from lanes. Activate scene lighting. Announce: "Medic 1 on scene — MVC, [X] vehicles, assessing." Check: fuel leaks (do not walk through), downed power lines (stage until utilities arrive), unstable vehicles (chock wheels, do not rock), fire risk. Never approach directly in front/rear of vehicle.
👥
Count & triage before treatingWalk the entire scene before treating anyone. Count patients: "Dispatch, Medic 1 — I have [X] patients at this MVC. Requesting [X] additional units." 2+ patients with 1 unit = immediately request mutual aid. Rapid START triage on multiple patients: walking → Green. Non-walking: breathing? rate? radial pulse? commands? → Red/Yellow/Black.
🦴
C-spine precautions — every MVCManual cervical stabilization on first contact — maintain until C-collar applied and patient is secured. High-mechanism indicators: rollover, ejection, intrusion >12 inches, fatality in same vehicle, unrestrained occupant, significant dash/steering column damage, T-bone at speed. Document neuro baseline before movement: "Grip strength equal bilaterally, sensation intact, no extremity pain."
📋
Primary survey — ABCDEAirway — jaw thrust (not head-tilt in trauma), suction, OPA if unconscious. Breathing — rate, depth, bilateral breath sounds — pneumothorax = absent sounds. Circulation — major external bleeds first: tourniquet for limb, hemostatic gauze for junctional, direct pressure for wounds. Tachycardia + hypotension = hemorrhagic shock. Disability — GCS, pupils, motor response. Expose — cut away clothing, assess full body.
🩹
Hemorrhage control — M of MARCHMassive hemorrhage before airway in tactical/trauma — applies here. Visible major bleeding: extremity wound → CAT tourniquet 2–3 inches proximal, note time. Junctional (groin, axilla, neck) → hemostatic gauze + sustained direct pressure 3+ minutes. Seat belt sign on abdomen = suspect internal hemorrhage.
💉
ALS — access & monitoring en routeIV x2 large-bore EN ROUTE — do not delay scene time for IV. NS TKO unless SBP <90 (→ 250mL bolus, reassess). 12-lead for any chest complaint — anterior STEMI may be trauma-unrelated. Fentanyl 1mcg/kg IV for pain per orders. IO if IV fails after 2 attempts on scene. Waveform capnography after advanced airway.
🩹
BLS — hemorrhage control, O2, request ALSTourniquet and wound packing are your most effective tools. O2 via NRB. Keep patient still and warm. Request ALS intercept immediately for any critical mechanism. If hypotensive and no spinal concern, elevate legs.
📦
Package & transport — scene time under 10 minStable seated patient: KED or vest device before move. Unstable or unconscious: rapid extrication — long board + 3-person log roll. Announce: "Medic 1 packaging — en route FMMC, ETA [X] min, GCS [X], [mechanism]." Critical trauma: load and go, treat en route.
📻
Radio report"FMMC, Medic 1 inbound — MVC, [restrained/unrestrained/ejected], GCS [X], BP [X/X], HR [X], SpO2 [X]%. [Suspected injuries: spinal/chest/abdominal/extremity]. Spinal precautions applied. ETA [X]."

Gunshot wound

🚫
Stage — wait for PD clearanceDo NOT enter until police clear the scene. Announce: "Medic 1 staging at [safe location], awaiting police clearance." Active shooter = stay in cold zone. If warm zone, use TECC/THREAT protocol — tourniquet and move, don't linger.
🩹
Hemorrhage control — MARCHMassive hemorrhage first. Limb wound → CAT tourniquet 2–3 inches proximal to wound — write time on tourniquet. Junctional/torso → hemostatic gauze packed deep into wound, not just over it. Maintain direct pressure minimum 3 minutes. Announce: "Tourniquet applied [time], left [extremity]."
🫁
Chest wounds — seal all sidesEvery penetrating chest wound gets an occlusive dressing — entry AND exit. Tension pneumothorax: tracheal deviation away from affected side, absent unilateral breath sounds, JVD, worsening hypotension despite fluid.
💨
ALS — needle decompressionTension pneumo signs → needle decompression: 2nd ICS midclavicular line, or 4th/5th ICS anterior axillary line. Announce: "# Needle decompression — [side]."
🔍
Full wound surveyLog-roll and expose entire body. Count all entry and exit wounds. Note trajectory for trauma team. Do not remove impaled objects — stabilize in place.
💉
ALS — vascular access & fluidsIV x2 large-bore en route. Permissive hypotension for penetrating trauma: target SBP 80–90. IO if IV fails. Announce: "# Large-bore IV x2, NS TKO."
🩹
BLS — hemorrhage control & transportTourniquet + wound packing are your primary tools. O2. Do not delay transport attempting IV. Request ALS intercept en route.
🏃
Load and goScene time target <10 min. Announce: "# Medic 1 en route — GSW [body location], bleeding [controlled/uncontrolled], [X] wounds, GCS [X], BP [X/X], ETA [X]."

Medical emergency / cardiac arrest

💓
Vitals first — narrate everythingVerbalize every reading: "BP 84 over 50, pulse 130 and thready, SpO2 88%, respirations 8 and shallow — that is a sick patient." Vital trends are more important than single readings. 12-lead early for any chest complaint or altered LOC. ST elevation = announce cath lab activation immediately en route.
📝
SAMPLE + OPQRST historySAMPLE: Signs/Symptoms → Allergies → Medications → Past Hx → Last oral intake → Events. OPQRST for pain: Onset (sudden vs gradual) → Provocation/palliation → Quality (sharp/pressure/burning) → Radiation (jaw, arm, back) → Severity (1–10) → Timing (constant vs intermittent). Ask family if patient cannot answer.
❤️
ALS — cardiac arrest protocolAnnounce: "Starting CPR [time]." Rate 100–120/min, depth 2–2.4 in, full recoil, minimize interruptions (<10 sec). Switch compressors q2 min. AED/defib pads immediately. Epi 1mg IV/IO q3–5 min. Amiodarone 300mg IV for refractory VF/pVT after 3rd shock. Document every rhythm check, shock, and medication.
❤️
BLS — cardiac arrestStart CPR immediately. 100–120/min, depth 2–2.4 in, hard and fast. AED as soon as available — follow prompts. Switch compressors every 2 min — announce switch: "Switching compressors." BVM 30:2 until AED arrival. Request ALS intercept immediately.
🫁
ALS — advanced airway & waveform capnographyBVM 30:2 until supraglottic placed. King LT / iGel allows continuous compressions. Waveform capnography: ETCO2 >10 = effective CPR. Sudden ETCO2 rise to 35–40 = ROSC — check pulse. ETCO2 persistently under 10 with good CPR = consider stopping resuscitation per protocol (physician orders).
🫁
BLS — airway managementOPA if unconscious and no gag. BVM 30:2 — two-rescuer preferred for seal. Do not hyperventilate. Do not pause CPR for more than 10 seconds. Keep everything going until ALS arrives.
🔄
Reversible causes — Hs and TsHs: Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hypo/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia. Ts: Tension pneumothorax (needle decompression ALS), Tamponade (transport), Toxins (Narcan/antidote), Thrombosis pulmonary (transport), Thrombosis coronary (ROSC → cath lab). Address the correctable ones on scene.
💊
ALS — chest pain protocol (non-arrest)Aspirin 324mg PO chewed. O2 if SpO2 <94%. Nitro 0.4mg SL — hold if SBP <90, inferior STEMI, or PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra/Cialis) in last 24–48hr. 12-lead: STEMI → Code STEMI to FMMC immediately. IV x2. Pain management per orders.
💊
BLS — chest painAspirin 324mg PO (chew, do not swallow whole). O2 if SpO2 <94%. Help patient self-administer their own nitro if prescribed and BP allows (>100 systolic). Position of comfort. Reassure — keep calm. Request ALS intercept.
📻
Radio report"FMMC, Medic 1 inbound — cardiac arrest / chest pain. CPR [in progress / ROSC at time X]. GCS [X], BP [X/X], HR [X], SpO2 [X]%. 12-lead [normal / STEMI pattern]. Interventions: [Epi, Amio, Narcan, glucose]. ETA [X]."

Fall / trauma

📏
Mechanism of injuryHeight >10 ft or >2× patient height = high mechanism. Surface type matters — concrete vs grass. Elderly: any fall = high mechanism regardless of height. Note MOI for hospital — it determines imaging needed. Announce MOI on radio.
🦴
Head-to-toe palpationSystematically palpate: skull → face → clavicles → chest/ribs → abdomen → pelvis (ONCE — do not repeat if unstable) → long bones. Crepitus, instability, deformity = suspected fracture. Announce findings: "Crepitus right femur — suspected fracture, applying traction splint."
⚡
Internal bleeding signsRigid or board-like abdomen = peritonitis. Distension = hemorrhage or bowel injury. Tachycardia + hypotension + narrowing pulse pressure = hemorrhagic shock. Grey-Turner sign (flank bruising) = retroperitoneal bleed. These patients need OR — rapid transport, IV fluid TKO.
🦺
Spinal packagingAnnounce before every patient movement. KED for ambulatory patients with neck/back pain. Long board: 3-person log roll — leader at head maintains neutral inline stabilization throughout. Announce: "On my count — log roll to right, one, two, three."
💉
ALS — IV access & pain managementIV x2 large-bore. NS 250mL bolus if SBP <90, reassess. Fentanyl 1mcg/kg IV or 2mcg/kg IN for pain per protocol. IO if IV fails. Do not delay transport for procedures.
🩹
BLS — splinting & comfortSplint fractures before moving. Traction splint for femur. O2. Keep patient warm. No IV or pain medication — request ALS intercept for significant pain or hypotension.

Overdose / unconscious person

🛡️
Scene safetyCheck for paraphernalia before touching anything. Do NOT touch loose powders — fentanyl analogs absorb transdermally and through mucous membranes. If white powder visible: gloves + mask, ventilate the space, request additional units.
🫁
Pattern recognitionOpioid: pinpoint pupils, RR <8, unconscious, cyanotic, gurgling. Stimulant (meth/cocaine): dilated pupils, tachycardia >120, hyperthermia, agitation, diaphoresis, chest pain. Benzo/alcohol: ataxia, slurred speech, normal pupils, responsive to sternal rub. Unknown: treat airway first — empiric Narcan is safe regardless of substance.
💊
Naloxone — opioid reversalNarcan 2mg IN (1mg per nostril). Pre-oxygenate with BVM if apneic before giving. Repeat q2–3min up to 10mg. Warn bystanders: patient may wake agitated — have LEO present if possible.
💉
ALS — IV Narcan & advanced airwayIV Narcan 0.4mg — titrate to respirations, not full reversal (prevents withdrawal agitation). King LT / iGel if BVM inadequate. IO if IV fails. Midazolam 2.5–5mg IM for stimulant-induced agitation with LEO restraint + medical orders.
💨
BLS — airway managementOPA if unconscious and no gag. BVM 10–12 breaths/min if RR <8 or apneic. Suction gurgling airways immediately. Recovery position once spontaneous breathing resumes. Request ALS intercept.
📻
Radio report"# [Hospital], Medic 1 inbound — suspected OD, [substance if known]. Narcan [X]mg given — [responsive/unresponsive]. RR [X], SpO2 [X]%, GCS [X]. ETA [X]."

Structure fire — victim rescue

⏱️
Stage at treatment sectorEMS does NOT enter burning structures. Stage minimum 100 ft, upwind. Announce: "Medic 1 staged at treatment sector — ready to receive patients." Identify and check in with Incident Command for assignment and patient handoff location.
🫁
Smoke inhalation — every patient100% O2 via NRB on every fire victim — even asymptomatic. Pulse ox is unreliable (cannot detect carboxyhemoglobin). Red flags: singed nasal hairs, hoarse voice, carbonaceous sputum, stridor, facial burns.
🚨
ALS — early airway managementAirway burns swell shut within minutes. Stridor or hoarseness → intubate NOW before edema develops. Do not wait for complete obstruction. Waveform capnography after intubation.
🚨
BLS — airway watchSigns of airway burns = immediate rapid transport + ALS intercept request. 100% O2 NRB. These airways can close within minutes — do not delay.
🔥
ALS — burns & fluid resuscitationRule of Nines: Head 9%, each arm 9%, each leg 18%, torso ant 18%, post 18%, perineum 1%. >20% BSA partial/full = critical. Parkland formula: 4ml × kg × %BSA burned (half in first 8h). IV x2. Dry sterile dressings — no creams, no ice.
🔥
BLS — burns managementRule of Nines for rough BSA estimate. Dry sterile dressings only — no creams, no ice, no wet dressings on large burns. Keep patient warm. Request ALS intercept for IV fluid resuscitation on significant burns.
O2 NRB 100%Burn dressings (dry)Hypothermia prevention Early intubationIV fluid resuscitationParkland formula ALS / air intercept for airway burns
🧠
Carbon monoxide poisoningCO symptoms: headache, nausea, confusion, vomiting with fire exposure. Cherry-red skin is a late and unreliable sign. 100% O2 immediately and continuously — halves CO half-life. Check ALL occupants inside the structure. Rapid transport — hyperbaric O2 may be indicated at hospital.

Stroke / CVA

🧠
Cincinnati / BE-FAST stroke screenBalance — sudden loss? Eyes — sudden vision changes? Face — ask to smile: one side drooping? Arm — hold both arms out 10 sec: one drifts? Speech — slurred, strange, or absent? Time — note exact onset or last known well time. Any ONE positive = stroke alert.
⏰
Time is braintPA window: 3–4.5 hours from last known well. Every minute of stroke = ~1.9 million neurons lost. Get exact onset from patient, family, or neighbors. Announce stroke alert to hospital early: "Stroke alert, [age/sex], last known well [time], positive facial droop and arm drift, BP [X/X]."
🔍
Critical assessmentsBlood glucose — hypoglycemia mimics stroke and is treatable. GCS. Cincinnati score. Pupils. BP (do not treat hypertension on scene unless SBP >220 — it's a compensatory mechanism). Do NOT give aspirin — hemorrhagic stroke = worse outcome with aspirin.
💉
InterventionsO2 only if SpO2 <94%. IV access en route (do not delay transport). Position of comfort — head of bed 0–15° for ischemic (promotes perfusion). Nothing by mouth. Glucose only if confirmed hypoglycemia — hyperglycemia worsens stroke outcome.
📻
Radio report"[Hospital], Medic 1 inbound — STROKE ALERT. [Age/sex], last known well [time]. Positive [facial droop / arm drift / speech]. BP [X/X], GCS [X], glucose [X]. ETA [X]."
O2 (if SpO2 <94%)GlucometerIV accessNO aspirin12-lead (rule out cardiac)

Anaphylaxis / severe allergic reaction

⚡
Recognize severity fastMild allergic reaction: localized hives/itching/redness only, no systemic involvement — Benadryl appropriate. Anaphylaxis = systemic involvement: airway (throat tightening, hoarse voice, stridor, tongue/lip swelling), bronchospasm (wheeze, cough, accessory muscle use), cardiovascular (hypotension, syncope, rapid weak pulse), GI (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, cramping), widespread hives/angioedema. One airway or cardiovascular symptom = anaphylaxis. Do not wait for "full picture." Anaphylaxis kills in minutes without epinephrine.
💉
Epinephrine — first, fast, no debateEpi 0.3mg IM (1:1000), anterolateral thigh — inject through clothing if needed. Do NOT delay for IV access. Do NOT give diphenhydramine first. Do NOT give Epi IV as first line (fatal risk). Announce: "Epinephrine 0.3mg IM administered [time]." If no improvement in 5–15 min: repeat Epi 0.3mg IM. A third dose may be given — do NOT withhold if patient is not responding. Most anaphylaxis deaths result from delayed Epi.
🫁
Airway & breathing — immediate threatO2 via NRB 15L — ALL anaphylaxis patients. Albuterol SVN 2.5mg nebulized if bronchospasm (wheeze, cough, SOB) — adjunct to Epi, not replacement. Watch for: stridor (upper airway edema closing), angioedema of tongue/lips/uvula. Stridor despite Epi = prepare for surgical airway — angioedema can make intubation impossible once edema develops. Position: supine + legs elevated for hypotension; sitting up for respiratory distress (let patient choose position of comfort).
❤️
Circulation — anaphylactic shockAnaphylaxis causes massive vasodilation + capillary leak — up to 50% of intravascular volume can shift to tissues within minutes. Signs: hypotension, tachycardia, syncope, altered LOC. Position supine + legs elevated. NS bolus 500mL–1L IV (ALS) — repeat PRN. If hypotension persists despite Epi + fluids: push-dose Epi 10–20mcg IV (ALS, with orders). Cardiac arrest from anaphylaxis: standard CPR + epinephrine q3–5min — but cause is distributive shock, so aggressive fluid resuscitation alongside.
🩺
Secondary treatments — adjuncts onlyDiphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25–50mg IV/IM — H1 blocker, treats hives/itching, does NOT treat bronchospasm or shock, does NOT replace Epi. Methylprednisolone 125mg IV — steroid, slow onset (4–6h), given to prevent biphasic reaction, not for acute treatment. Ranitidine 50mg IV (H2 blocker) — adjunct if available. Do not delay or substitute Epi for any of these medications.
⚠️
Biphasic anaphylaxis — mandatory hospital obsBiphasic reaction: symptoms resolve, then return 1–8 hours later (sometimes up to 72h) — without re-exposure to allergen. Occurs in ~20% of anaphylaxis cases. Cannot be predicted by initial severity. ALL anaphylaxis patients require hospital observation — minimum 4–6 hours. Patients who want to leave AMA must be informed explicitly of biphasic risk. Document the education given.
🩹
BLS — Epi auto-injector, O2, transportEpiPen (0.3mg adult, 0.15mg Jr) — anterolateral thigh. Patient's own auto-injector is the most common scenario. Assist patient if they cannot self-administer. O2 NRB. Position of comfort. Request ALS intercept — these patients need IV access, repeat Epi, fluids. Rapid transport regardless of initial response — biphasic risk.

OB emergency / imminent delivery

⏰
Transport or deliver?If crowning visible = deliver on scene. If contractions <2 min apart AND >10 min to hospital = prepare on scene. Announce: "Medic 1 on scene — imminent delivery, not transporting at this time."
👶
Normal deliverySupport head — do NOT pull. Check for nuchal cord (cord around neck): if loose, slip over head; if tight, double-clamp and cut before delivery. Guide anterior shoulder down then posterior shoulder up. Dry and stimulate infant immediately after delivery. Assign Apgar at 1 and 5 minutes.
✂️
Cord, placenta & PPHClamp cord at 4 inches and 6 inches from infant — cut between. Placenta delivers 5–30 min post-birth — do NOT pull cord. If excessive bleeding after delivery (PPH): uterine fundal massage, oxytocin 10u IM if available. Keep placenta — send to hospital.
🆘
ComplicationsBreech (feet/buttocks first): support, do NOT pull, rapid transport. Cord prolapse: elevate presenting part off cord, do NOT push cord back, rapid transport in knee-chest position. Shoulder dystocia: McRoberts (hyperflex hips to chest) + suprapubic pressure — never fundal pressure.
📻

Drowning / near-drowning

🛡️
Scene safety — reach, throw, don't goDo NOT enter water without rescue training and equipment. Use rope, pole, or PFD from shore. Request water rescue team. C-spine precautions only if witnessed dive or jump mechanism.
🫁
Airway is the priorityDrowning injury is hypoxia — oxygenation is the entire treatment. Begin rescue breaths immediately for apneic patient once out of water. Do NOT attempt to drain lungs — it is ineffective and delays oxygenation. 5 rescue breaths before compressions (modified protocol for drowning).
❤️
CPR if pulselessDrowning arrest is usually hypoxic before cardiac — oxygenation is as important as compressions. AED as soon as available — hypothermic heart may be in VF. Do not withhold resuscitation based on submersion time in cold water: "Not dead until warm and dead."
🌡️
Hypothermia managementRemove all wet clothing immediately — cut if needed. Active rewarming: warm blankets to trunk, warm IV NS if available. Warm humidified O2. Handle gently — cold myocardium is irritable, can trigger VF with rough movement.
📻

Stab wound / impalement

🚫
Scene safetyStage until PD clears scene. Edged-weapon scenes can re-escalate quickly. Announce: "Medic 1 staging, awaiting PD clearance."
🩹
Do NOT remove impaled objectsStabilize impaled objects in place — removing them can release tamponaded hemorrhage and cause rapid exsanguination. Use bulky dressings around the object to immobilize it. Exception: object preventing CPR on chest — remove only in cardiac arrest.
🫁
Chest & abdominal woundsChest penetration: occlusive dressing same as GSW protocol. Evisceration (bowel outside body): do NOT push back in — cover with moist sterile dressing and dry outer dressing. Abdominal wounds bleed internally — watch for shock signs.
💉
Hemorrhage control & accessHemostatic gauze for deep wounds — pack don't cover. CAT tourniquet for extremity wounds. IV x2 large-bore. Permissive hypotension (SBP 80–90) for abdominal/thoracic penetrating wounds. Rapid transport — these patients need OR.

Mass casualty incident (MCI)

Activates alert mode — flashing banner + live triage board

📻
Declare MCI immediately"Dispatch, Medic 1 — declaring MCI at [location]. Estimating [X] patients. Requesting [X] additional units, police, fire, and supervisor to establish Incident Command." Do NOT begin treatment until size-up is complete. Your first job is information gathering.
🏃
START triage — tag, don't treatWalk the scene quickly. Walking? → Green (tag and move to assembly point). Not walking: open airway — breathing? No → Black. Yes → RR >30 or <10 → Red. Radial pulse absent → Red. Won't follow simple commands → Red. Otherwise → Yellow. One tourniquet on arterial bleeds only. Move on.
📍
Sector setupTreatment area: Red sector (immediate) and Yellow sector (delayed) separated. Transport sector for loading. Command post uphill/upwind. Each sector needs a sector leader reporting to Medical Branch Director → Incident Commander. Do NOT freelance — work your sector only.
🚑
Treatment prioritiesRed first: airway and bleeding only — load and go. Yellow: monitor and prepare for transport. Green: self-treat or bystander-assist. Black: deceased — document tag number, do not disturb scene. Track every patient: tag number, triage level, transport unit, destination hospital.

Diabetic emergency

🩸
Blood glucose — check immediatelyBG is your first action. BG <60 mg/dL = hypoglycemia. BG >300 mg/dL = possible DKA/HHS. Narrate: "BG is [X] — treating for hypoglycemia." Do NOT assume cause of altered LOC without checking glucose first.
🧁
Hypoglycemia treatmentConscious and can swallow: 15–20g oral glucose (gel, juice, glucose tablets). Unconscious or cannot protect airway: do NOT give PO. IV/IM route only.
💉
ALS — D50 / GlucagonD50 25g IV push — reassess BG in 10 min. If no IV: Glucagon 1mg IM (slower onset — warn family patient may vomit). Thiamine 100mg IV before glucose if alcoholism suspected (Wernicke's prevention).
💉
BLS — glucose + ALS interceptOral glucose only if conscious. Request ALS intercept if unconscious — they need IV D50. Keep airway open. Reassess BG q10 min after treatment.
📈
Hyperglycemia / DKABG >300 + fruity breath + Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid) + dehydration = DKA. IV NS for volume. Do NOT give insulin prehospital. Transport — DKA requires hospital management.
📻

Pediatric emergency

📏
Broselow tape — use it every timeMeasure from head to heel. Tape color determines weight-based doses and equipment sizes. Narrate the color: "Patient is in the [green/yellow/red] zone — approximately [X] kg." Never guess weight on a child.
📊
Pediatric vital signs — know your normalsInfant (0–1): HR 120–160, RR 30–60. Toddler (1–3): HR 90–150, RR 24–40. School age (6–12): HR 70–120, RR 18–30. Tachycardia is the FIRST sign of shock in children — BP drops late. Hypotension in a child = decompensated shock.
🫁
Airway — pediatric differencesLarge occiput causes airway flexion when supine — use shoulder roll. Tongue is proportionally larger. Cricoid is narrowest (no cuff needed in small children). BVM: 2-person preferred — E-C clamp. Rate: infant 12–20 breaths/min, child 12–20. Do NOT hyperventilate.
🩹
Shock in pediatric patientsSigns: tachycardia, pallor, mottling, prolonged cap refill (>2 sec), altered LOC. BP normal until >30% volume loss. NS bolus 20 mL/kg over 5–10 min — reassess after each bolus. Max 3 boluses without hospital guidance.
💉
ALS — IO access in pediatricIV access failure after 2 attempts — go IO immediately (tibial plateau preferred under age 5). IO is the standard of care in pediatric resuscitation. Confirm placement: aspirate marrow, flush with saline — resistance = misplaced.
⚠️
Non-accidental trauma (NAT)Bruising in non-mobile infant, fractures in different healing stages, retinal hemorrhage, patterned burns, delayed presentation = report to hospital staff and document objectively. Do NOT accuse parent on scene. Document injuries and mechanism provided.

Burns — thermal / chemical / electrical

🚫
Stop the burning processRemove patient from source. Remove ALL clothing and jewelry — exception: burned-on clothing (do NOT pull). Chemical burns: brush off dry chemical first, then flush 20–30 min continuous water. Electrical: do NOT touch patient until power source confirmed off — treat scene as unsafe until confirmed.
📐
Rule of Nines — BSA estimationHead 9%, each arm 9%, each anterior leg 9%, each posterior leg 9%, anterior torso 18%, posterior torso 18%, perineum 1%. Palm of patient's hand ≈ 1% BSA — use it for irregular burn shapes. In pediatric patients: head is proportionally larger (18%) and legs smaller. Document: location, depth, total BSA %.
🌡️
Burn depth assessmentSuperficial (1st degree): red, dry, painful, no blisters — no prehospital dressing needed, reassure patient. Partial thickness (2nd degree): blistered, moist, extremely painful, bright red or pale pink — cover with dry sterile dressing, do NOT pop blisters. Full thickness (3rd degree): leathery, white/brown/black/charred, painless (nerves destroyed) — always critical.
🔥
Critical burn indicators — immediate transportAny single one = load and go: face/neck/hands/feet/genitalia burned, >20% BSA partial/full thickness, full thickness ANY area, circumferential burns (limb or chest), inhalation injury signs (soot in airway, hoarse voice, singed nasal hairs, carbonaceous sputum), electrical mechanism, chemical exposure to face/eyes, burn + blast injury.
⚡
Electrical burns — hidden injuryEntry and exit wounds are only the surface — the internal track through tissue is the real injury. Look for: cardiac dysrhythmia (all electrical contacts need 12-lead), rhabdomyolysis (myoglobinuria — dark "tea" colored urine), spinal injury from tetanic muscle contraction, occult fractures. Even low-voltage contacts can cause VF. All electrical burn patients need hospital evaluation.
☣️
Chemical burns — decontaminationFlush continuously with large volumes of room-temperature water for minimum 20–30 min. Dry chemical: brush off before water. Hydrofluoric acid (industrial): calcium gluconate gel if available — penetrates deep and can cause fatal hypocalcemia. Alkalis (lye, cement) are worse than acids — continue flushing. Protect yourself — secondary contamination risk. Eyes: flush with NS via Morgan lens if available.
🩹
Circumferential burns — eschar watchCircumferential full-thickness burns create a rigid eschar (dead tissue) that can act as a tourniquet as swelling increases underneath. Limb: assess distal pulses q5 min — loss = compartment syndrome emergency. Chest: eschar can prevent chest wall expansion — watch for worsening respiratory failure. Both require emergent escharotomy at hospital — notify early.
💉
ALS — fluids, airway & painParkland formula: 4mL × kg × %BSA = 24hr total (half in first 8hr from TIME OF INJURY, not arrival). IV NS large-bore x2. Airway burns: if stridor or hoarse voice — RSI NOW before edema closes the airway. Fentanyl for pain — burns are extremely painful. Morphine avoided in hypotensive patients.
🩹
BLS — cover, comfort, transportDry sterile dressings only — no creams, butter, toothpaste, ice, or wet dressings on large burns (hypothermia risk). Prevent hypothermia — burns destroy thermoregulation. O2 NRB. Oral analgesics only per protocol. ALS intercept for >10% BSA, inhalation injury, or electrical mechanism.
🌡️
Hypothermia — major threatBurned skin cannot regulate temperature. Large BSA burns lose heat rapidly. Avoid wet dressings, cold water, exposed skin. Cover patient with dry blankets. Warmed IV fluids if available (ALS). Hypothermia worsens outcomes significantly — treat it as aggressively as the burn itself.

Seizure

⏱️
Time the seizure — criticalNote exact onset time. Announce: "Seizure start [time]." Status epilepticus = >5 minutes or two seizures without full recovery. Seizure duration drives your treatment decisions.
🛡️
Protect — do NOT restrainClear the area of hard objects. Cushion the head. Do NOT put anything in the patient's mouth. Tongue biting is painful but not life-threatening — airway obstruction from restraint is. Loosen tight clothing.
🔄
Recovery position post-ictalAfter seizure stops: turn lateral to prevent aspiration. Suction if secretions or vomiting. O2 via NRB. Assess airway adequacy. GCS will be depressed — normal post-ictal. Document return to baseline.
🩸
Check glucoseHypoglycemia causes seizures. Check BG immediately post-ictal. BG <60 = treat. Also assess for: trauma from fall (head strike, C-spine concern), fever (febrile seizure in peds), medications taken/missed, substance use.
💊
ALS — status epilepticus treatmentMidazolam 10mg IM (preferred — fastest) or 5mg IV/IO, or Diazepam 5–10mg IV. Repeat once if no response. If still seizing after benzodiazepine: rapid transport — may need phenobarbital or propofol in ED. Document all meds given.
🩹
BLS — supportive care + ALSProtect, position, oxygenate. Oral glucose if BG <60 and conscious. Request ALS intercept for status epilepticus — they have the benzodiazepines. Transport all first-time seizures regardless of recovery.

Psychiatric / behavioral emergency

🛡️
Scene safety first — alwaysDo NOT approach a potentially violent patient without LEO on scene. Stage until PD clears. Assess for weapons. Announce: "Medic 1 staging — awaiting law enforcement clearance." Even calm-appearing psych patients can escalate unpredictably. Low-stimulation environment: reduce responders present, lower voices, step back.
🔍
Rule out organic / medical cause FIRSTPsychiatric behavior can be caused by: hypoglycemia (check BG immediately), CO poisoning, head trauma, stroke, seizure, hypoxia, substance intoxication, medication effect. These are medical emergencies — don't assume it's purely behavioral. AEIOU TIPS: Alcohol, Epilepsy, Insulin, Overdose, Uremia — Trauma, Infection, Psychiatric, Stroke.
💬
De-escalation approachIntroduce yourself calmly: "Hi, I'm [name] with EMS. I'm here to help, not to hurt you." Non-threatening posture: hands visible, don't crowd, stay at eye level if possible. Speak slowly and clearly — one question at a time. Validate emotions without agreeing with delusions: "That sounds really frightening — I can see you're upset." Give patient some sense of control: "Would you like to sit down?"
⚠️
Recognize escalation warning signsPacing, clenching fists, raised voice, scanning for exits, sudden silence, moving toward you. If any of these: increase distance, call for LEO, do not turn your back. Tactical retreat is not failure — it is safety.
💊
ALS — pharmacological interventionOnly after LEO has physical control. Excited Delirium (ExDS) — hot, strong, confused, not responding to pain: Midazolam 10mg IM (vastus lateralis, can give through clothing). Agitated but cooperative: Midazolam 2.5–5mg IM or IV. Monitor airway closely post-sedation — respiratory depression risk. 12-lead if cardiac symptoms or history.
🩹
BLS — de-escalate, observe, request ALSNo chemical restraint in BLS scope. Document behavior objectively. Request ALS intercept if combative, agitated, or medical cause suspected. Ensure LEO is present before approach. Protect yourself — leave the room if unsure.
📋
Baker Act / WI 51.15 — psychiatric holdWisconsin 51.15 — Emergency Detention: person may be detained by law enforcement if: danger to self or others due to mental illness, AND is unwilling/unable to accept voluntary treatment. EMS role: document behavior observed, request LEO initiate hold if criteria met, transport to appropriate receiving facility. Always document specific behaviors seen — not just "patient was acting crazy."
📝
RP — documentation languageObjective only. Use: "Patient stated [exact quote]." "Observed patient [specific behavior]." "Patient was pacing, clenching fists, yelling 'get away from me' repeatedly." Avoid: "Patient was crazy / irrational / out of it." Your report may be used for a legal commitment proceeding — be precise.

Animal attack / bite

🛡️
Scene safety — animal still present?Do NOT approach until animal is confirmed contained or gone. Domestic animal attacks: request animal control if animal is still on scene. Wild animal: stage until law enforcement clears. Do NOT enter to retrieve a patient if the animal is active — announce staging and await containment. A second victim helps no one.
🩸
Hemorrhage control firstAnimal attacks can produce severe lacerations and puncture wounds — arterial hemorrhage is possible. Apply direct pressure immediately. Extremity: CAT tourniquet if uncontrolled arterial bleed. Neck/face/scalp: manual pressure with hemostatic gauze — no tourniquet options here. Large dog bites can fracture underlying bone — treat wound AND assess for orthopedic injury.
🔍
Wound assessment — bites are deceptivePuncture wounds appear small but can track deep. Assess for: underlying fracture (especially dog bites to hands/forearms in children), tendon or neurovascular involvement, evisceration in large predator attacks, degloving injuries. Do NOT probe the wound on scene. Document exact location, size, and depth estimate.
🧫
Infection risk — all bites are contaminatedAnimal bites carry extremely high infection risk: dog saliva contains Pasteurella, Capnocytophaga (especially dangerous in immunocompromised or asplenic patients — can be fatal). Cat bites are particularly infectious despite appearing minor — deep punctures inoculate bacteria under skin. Irrigate copiously with saline if available, but wound closure is a hospital decision. Document last tetanus immunization.
🦠
Rabies risk — document everythingAny unprovoked wild animal attack has rabies risk: bats, raccoons, foxes, skunks, coyotes — highest risk. Dogs and cats: rabies possible, especially unvaccinated or stray. Domestic livestock: lower risk. Document: animal species, provoked vs unprovoked behavior, animal's condition (sick-acting, daytime bat exposure), animal's fate (escaped, contained, deceased). All info is critical for rabies post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) decision at hospital.
👶
Pediatric — higher severity patternChildren are at greatest risk for head and neck bites (short stature = face at dog's mouth level). Pediatric skull is thinner — dog bites to the head can penetrate cranium. Assess neuro status. Scalp lacerations bleed profusely — do not underestimate blood loss. Prioritize airway (potential neck injury) and hemorrhage.
💉
ALS — hemorrhage, airway, painLarge-vessel neck bite: two-finger pressure over vessel, do NOT clamp blindly. IV x2 if significant bleeding. Fentanyl for pain. Airway compromise from neck bite or swelling: early intubation. Fluid resuscitation for hemorrhagic shock. 12-lead if significant blood loss (compensatory tachycardia).
🩹
BLS — control, cover, transportControl bleeding with direct pressure. Moist sterile dressing on wounds. Do NOT attempt wound closure or deep irrigation in field. Splint extremity if fracture suspected. ALS intercept for arterial hemorrhage, airway involvement, or pediatric head/neck bite.

Traffic stop — decision guide

Grounds to stop a vehicle

  • Moving violation observed (speed, signal, lane)
  • Equipment violation (lights, plates, inspection)
  • Plate returns stolen / suspended owner
  • Reasonable suspicion of criminal activity
  • Active warrant on registered owner
  • Reckless / erratic driving behavior
  • OWI indicators (weaving, speed fluctuation)

Grounds to arrest (traffic context)

  • OWI confirmed by FST + PBT
  • Active arrest warrant via NCIC
  • Felony charge (fleeing, hit-and-run w/ injury)
  • Driving while revoked (felony level)
  • Stolen vehicle confirmed
  • Contraband discovered via PC / K9
  • Witness / victim flagging suspect vehicle

Citation vs. warning guide

Write Warning

  • 1st offense, cooperative driver
  • Minor speed (1–9 over)
  • Equipment fix-it issues
  • Clean record returned from NCIC
  • Good faith / emergency circumstances

Use Discretion

  • 10–19 mph over — consider context
  • Prior warnings → bump to citation
  • Minor seatbelt first offense
  • Cooperative + clean record = favorable
  • Night / low traffic / open road

Must Cite / Arrest

  • 20+ mph over — mandatory citation
  • Reckless driving confirmed
  • OWI indicators present
  • Active warrant returned
  • Fleeing / eluding — arrest eligible
  • Hit and run

Officer positioning & safety

During stop

  • Stop vehicle 1–2 car lengths back, offset right
  • Approach from driver-side B-pillar angle
  • Observe occupants through rear window as you walk up
  • Call out plate / make / model before exiting

High-risk indicators

  • Multiple occupants, nervous movement
  • Hands not visible / reaching under seat
  • Plate mismatch to vehicle type
  • Stolen vehicle return from NCIC
  • Driver door opens before vehicle stopped
  • → Consider felony stop protocol

⚔️ Fire Attack Strategy — choose one on arrival

🟢 OFFENSIVE

When: Fire is survivable, victims may be inside, structure is sound, water is available.

  • Interior crew advances attack line directly to seat of fire
  • Simultaneous primary search
  • SCBA required — full PPE
  • RIC team staged and ready
  • Announce entry: "Engine 1 going offensive, Side Alpha."
  • PAR every 10 min or at each benchmark

Best chance of victim rescue. Highest crew risk.

🟡 TRANSITIONAL

When: Heavy fire condition — cool fire from exterior first, then convert to offensive for search.

  • Blitz attack: exterior master stream or 2½" to knock fire volume quickly
  • Do NOT make entry until fire knocked from outside
  • Once steam and smoke clear: switch to offensive interior ops
  • Announce: "Engine 1 transitional — blitz attack, Side Charlie."
  • Reassess structure integrity before entry
  • Time-sensitive — don't stay exterior too long if victims inside

Buys time. Reduces conditions before crew entry.

🔴 DEFENSIVE

When: Structure beyond saving, no savable victims, collapse risk, or insufficient resources.

  • ALL crews operate from exterior — no interior entry
  • Master streams and deck guns to suppress fire
  • Protect exposures (adjacent buildings)
  • Announce: "[Address] Command going defensive — all units evacuate the structure. PAR immediately."
  • Establish collapse zone: 1.5× building height minimum
  • Monitor for extension to exposures

Crew safety first. Structure is a write-off.

⚠️ Strategy change: When converting from Offensive → Defensive, announce clearly and call PAR before any crew transitions: "All units, [Address] Command — converting to defensive. All interior crews exit NOW. PAR on exit."

Wildland / brush fire

📻
On-scene report — LCES first"Engine 1 on scene — [location], [acreage estimate], [fuel type], fire heading [direction], wind [speed/direction]. Establishing [location] Command." Immediately establish LCES before any crew deployment: Lookout → Communications → Escape Routes → Safety Zones. Announce escape route to all crews before commitment. Give acreage estimate — even a rough one helps dispatch resources.
🔥
Fire behavior — the basics that killFire runs uphill — slope doubles spread rate every 10° of steepening. Never position crews above the fire. Wind triples spread rate — wind shifts are the most common cause of firefighter fatalities. Canyons, chimneys, and saddles concentrate and accelerate fire dramatically. Head fire (burning into wind) = most dangerous. Flank (sides) and heel (rear) are safer attack positions. Spot fires ahead of the main fire mean fire is running — announce and disengage immediately.
🌡️
Fuel types & fire behaviorLight fuels (grass, annual weeds): ignite easily, burn fast, extinguish faster — use direct attack when practical. Medium fuels (brush, shrubs, chaparral): intense heat, spread moderately, ladder fuels that carry fire into tree crowns. Heavy fuels (logs, stumps, duff): slow ignition, long burning, deep-seated — require extensive mop-up. Crown fire: fire in tree canopy, wind-driven — extremely dangerous, near impossible to suppress directly; focus on flanks and ahead of fire. Moisture content below 10% = extreme fire behavior.
⚔️
Attack methodsDirect attack: Attack fire at the edge — most effective but requires close proximity to flames. Suitable for low-intensity, early morning, light fuel fires. Use hand lines, hand tools, engines at edge. Indirect attack: Build control lines away from fire edge (fireline, dozer line, road) and burn out fuels between line and fire. Use when direct is too dangerous or intense. Parallel attack: Lines built near but not at fire edge — balance between direct and indirect. Best for moderate intensity. Announce attack method to IC: "Engine 1 direct attack, north flank."
🔨
Hand tools & fireline constructionPulaski: combination axe/hoe — chop and scrape. McLeod: rake and hoe — scrape and clear. Shovel: dirt throwing, smothering, line construction. Combi tool: saw + hoe. Standard fireline: scrape to mineral soil, 2× the flame length wide at minimum. Mark fireline ends with flagging. Burnout: lighting fire inside control line to consume fuel — requires IC approval and wind must carry burnout into main fire.
🚒
Engine operationsStructure engines on wildland: limited offroad capability, protect tires from heat. Wildland engines (Type 3–7): high clearance, pump-and-roll capability. Pump-and-roll: apply water while vehicle moves along fire edge — most effective tool in light fuel. Never park in unburned fuel without escape route confirmed. Turn engine around before exiting — never back into fire. If overrun: shelter in place inside cab (do not run).
🏃
Retreat & safety zones — non-negotiableSafety zone: large enough for all personnel + apparatus with 30+ ft spacing. Cleared mineral soil or previously burned area preferred. NEVER use a safety zone that requires driving through the fire to reach. Announce: "All units — disengage to safety zone at [location]. Escape route is [road]." Fire shelter: last resort only — dig a depression if time allows, face down with feet toward fire, hold shelter down against wind. Do not run if overtaken — deploy shelter immediately.
💧
Structure defense (WUI)Wildland-Urban Interface: clear 30 ft defensible space, close all vents/eaves/windows (ember entry point). Wet structure and roof. Leave doors unlocked. Shut off gas at meter. Set up and leave before fire arrives — do NOT shelter inside structure during fire front. Structures burning from inside out = ember intrusion. After fire front passes (2–10 min): quickly move to suppress small fires on structure. Announce when departing: "Engine 1 departing [address] — structure defense complete."
✈️
Air resourcesAirtanker (SEAT/VLAT): retardant drop to slow fire spread — gives ground crews time to build line. Not a direct suppression tool. Helicopter with bucket/tank: water drops on active flame, crew transport, recon. Announce LZ: 100×100 ft minimum, no wires overhead, wind direction flagged. Clear LZ of all personnel before aircraft arrival. Do NOT key radio within 100 ft of aircraft. All air requests through IC to air attack supervisor (ATGS).
🌙
Night operationsFire behavior changes at night: cooler temps and higher humidity slow fire — best window for line construction. However: crew fatigue, reduced visibility, missed hazards (snags, terrain). Night attack rules: minimum two-person crews, headlamps mandatory, marked escape routes with flagging/lights, radio check every 30 min. Snags (standing dead trees) are highest night hazard — fall without warning. Announce all crew deployments to IC at night.
🌡️
Red flag conditions — disengage triggerRed Flag Warning = weather criteria met for extreme fire behavior: temps 90°F+, relative humidity <15%, sustained winds 15+ mph, or any wind gust 25+ mph. On Red Flag days: increase escape route frequency, reduce direct attack, pre-position at safety zones. IC may restrict interior operations. Spot fires during Red Flag = disengage immediately. Announce: "Command — Red Flag conditions, all crews to safety zones pending IC approval to continue."

Water rescue

📻
On-scene report & reach/throw/row/go"Engine 1 on scene — [location], water rescue, [surface / swift water / ice], victim [in water / submerged]. Establishing command. Swift water team requested." Response priority: Reach → Throw → Row → Go (enter as LAST resort, requires swift water certification).
🌊
Swift water hazardsStrainers (debris across current) trap victims underwater instantly. Foot entrapment in moving water causes drowning even in shallow water — never stand up in swift current over knee deep. Hydraulics (recirculating currents below dams or drops) re-circulate victims. Announce current speed estimate and downstream hazards to all units. No crew in swift water without swift water PPE (dry suit, PFD, helmet, knife).
🧊
Ice rescue100 ft safety rope on all rescuers. Ice rescuer uses ice picks, dry suit, PFD. Throw rope first — if victim can self-rescue, do not need to send rescuer. Ice condition: clear blue ice = strongest; white/opaque = weakened. Ladder laid flat distributes weight — slide toward victim. Do not run on ice. Hypothermia protocol on extrication: cold water victims rarely show signs of life until warm — "not dead until warm and dead."
🏊
Surface water / pond / lakePFD on all rescuers in or near water. Defensive swimming posture if swept in: feet downstream, on back, toes up. Boat operations: slow approach to avoid swamping victim. Announce when victim is contact-positive: "Contact positive — beginning tow to [shore side]." Hypothermia assessment on shore — alert EMS.
🤿
Submerged victimNote submersion time — cold water increases survival window (documented cases to 90 min in cold water). Mark last seen point. Dive team request: surface water recovery vs dive recovery depends on visibility and depth. Do NOT enter murky water without dive equipment. Body recovery is non-emergency ops — crew safety always first.

HazMat response

📻
On-scene report & isolation"Engine 1 on scene — [address], possible HazMat. [Odor/vapor/spill/fire]. Vehicle/placard [UN number if visible from distance]. Requesting HazMat team. Isolating [ERG distance] and denying entry." Do NOT approach unidentified spill or unknown odor without at minimum Level C PPE. Distance is the first defense — look up ERG initial isolation distance immediately.
📖
Identify the material — TRACEM-PUse TRACEM-P mnemonic to assess hazard type: Thermal, Radiological, Asphyxiation, Chemical, Etiological (biological), Mechanical, Psychological. ID sources: placard → UN number → ERG guide → MSDS/SDS on-site → CHEMTREC (1-800-424-9300). Shipping papers are in the cab of any truck. Announce material and ERG guide number to all units immediately.
🟢
Hot / warm / cold zonesHot zone: contaminated/IDLH — Level A or B only, SCBA mandatory. Warm zone: decon corridor — appropriate PPE, potential splash/vapor exposure. Cold zone: CP, EMS, media, staging — no PPE required. Announce zone boundaries with landmarks: "Hot zone is 300 ft radius around tanker — warm zone decon set up at north fence line." Never compress zones under time pressure.
🚿
DecontaminationAll hot zone personnel exit through decon before entering warm/cold. Emergency decon: strip outer clothing (removes ~80%), copious water flush. Technical decon: wash → rinse → wash cycle, monitor at exit. Victim decon before transport — notify hospital: "Patient decontaminated, [substance], monitor for [symptoms/exposure route]." Runoff is hazardous — capture if possible, notify EPA/DNR if enters storm drain.
☢️
Radiological incidentsRequest radiation dosimetry immediately. Time/Distance/Shielding are controls: minimize time in zone, maximize distance, use shielding (vehicle, building). Alpha particles: blocked by paper/skin — ingestion/inhalation is the hazard. Beta: blocked by thin metal or plastic. Gamma: penetrating radiation — requires dense shielding (lead, concrete). Monitor personnel dose. Never exceed dose limits — rotate crews. Notify state radiological response team.
🔥
Flammable / reactive materialsFlammable solids: self-heating or water-reactive — do NOT apply water to metal fires (sodium, magnesium, lithium) — use Class D extinguisher or dry sand. Flammable liquids: foam suppression, do not flush into drains. Oxidizers: accelerate fire dramatically — increase hose flow and distance. Radioactive solids: handle as contamination + radiation hazard simultaneously. Cryogenic liquids: extremely cold, vapors heavier than air, displace O2 in low areas.

Rapid Intervention Crew (RIC)

The RIC (also called IRIT — Initial Rapid Intervention Team) exists for one purpose: to rescue a downed or trapped firefighter. The RIC is always staged and ready at any working interior fire. They do not take other assignments until a Mayday is declared.

🧰
RIC composition & stagingMinimum two firefighters (typically three or four for large structures). Staged at command post or designated entry point — never inside the fire building. Fully dressed out in full PPE and SCBA. RIC carries dedicated rescue bag. Announce to IC: "Engine 3 — RIC is established at Side Alpha, four personnel." IC acknowledges and logs RIC location. RIC stays on primary radio channel — monitor all traffic.
🎒
RIC bag contentsMinimum RIC bag: extra SCBA cylinder (or airline), SCBA mask, hand light, TIC (thermal imaging camera), rope / webbing, wire cutters, door chocks, forcible entry tool (halligan), lifeline / tag line 150 ft, personal escape system. Some departments carry air monitoring equipment and medical kit. Know where every item is — you'll be using it in zero-visibility conditions.
📻
Mayday received — RIC activationIC declares: "MAYDAY declared — RIC activate. RIC enter [Side Alpha]. All other traffic hold." RIC leader acknowledges: "RIC copy — entering Side Alpha." RIC enters with a dedicated guide rope (lifeline) from entry point. Do NOT freelance — work off the lifeline. Announce every major turn and landmark on the guide rope. IC moves all other radio traffic to Tac 2 to keep Mayday channel clear.
🔍
Locating the downed firefighterUse TIC to scan for heat signatures. Listen for PASS device alarm (continuous tone). Call out: "RIC — firefighter down, call out!" Check last known location reported in Mayday. If downed FF has radio: ask for light flash, PASS activation, or sound. Search systematically — do NOT spread out without guide rope. Once located: announce immediately: "RIC — firefighter located, Division 2, Side Bravo."
🤝
Rescue & packagingCheck FF's SCBA: is regulator connected? Air supply? If air depleted: connect RIC airline or share regulator (buddy breathing only as last resort). If FF is conscious: guide out on lifeline — they walk if possible. If unconscious: use drag rescue device (DRD) on turnout coat or webbing harness. Announce movement: "RIC moving victim toward Alpha — need EMS at entry." Do NOT stop to do medical care inside structure — move first, treat outside.
⚠️
RIC air managementRIC must manage their own air supply carefully — two rescuers + one victim = significant air drain. Rule of air management: 1/3 in, 1/3 out, 1/3 reserve. If RIC air gets low before rescue complete: announce immediately to IC — second RIC team must be staged and ready. Never make a Mayday worse by making a second Mayday. Announce: "RIC air low — need relief team to entry."
🚨
If RIC is in distressRIC member transmits MAYDAY: "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY — RIC team, [location], [problem], [air status]." IC activates second RIC or requests mutual aid RIC immediately. This is a compounding Mayday — IC must announce to all units and reassign resources. IC does NOT send more crews into a structural collapse or imminent collapse zone — waits for structure evaluation.

HazMat Team

The HazMat Team is a specialized unit trained to identify, contain, and mitigate chemical, biological, radiological, and flammable/reactive material incidents. First-due engines establish isolation and deny entry — they do NOT attempt mitigation without the HazMat Team on scene.

🚒
First-due role (pre-HazMat team)Identify material from safe distance: placard, UN number, container shape. Look up ERG guide. Establish isolation perimeter per ERG initial isolation distance. Deny entry — no one in without proper PPE. Request HazMat Team through dispatch. Announce upwind/uphill staging. Notify dispatch of any exposures (people affected, drains, waterways). Document time of release estimate if known.
🧪
HazMat team operationsTeam Leader conducts entry briefing: material ID, concentration, PPE level, entry objectives, time limit, decon procedure, and emergency signals. Entry team (minimum two) enters hot zone with buddy system — never solo. Monitoring team at hot zone perimeter. Decon team at warm zone exit. Safety Officer independent of entry team. All entries logged with time in and time out.
🛡️
PPE selection by scenarioUnknown material / vapor hazard / high concentration → Level A (encapsulating suit + SCBA). Liquid splash / known material at lower vapor risk → Level B (splash suit + SCBA). Known material / O₂ adequate / no vapor skin hazard → Level C (coverall + APR). Cold zone monitoring / no exposure risk → Level D (turnout gear). PPE selection is a team decision — never self-upgrade or self-downgrade without HazMat team approval.
🚿
Decon operationsDecon team runs warm zone corridor. Gross decon: emergency removal of outer clothing + water flush (for symptomatic victims — immediate). Technical decon: wash → rinse → wash (3-step minimum). Personnel decon: all entry team members decon on exit regardless of exposure. Monitor decon effectiveness with meters. Victim decon priority: ambulatory → non-ambulatory → deceased. Notify receiving hospital of substance and decon performed.
☢️
Radiological responseRadiation control officer notified immediately. Issue dosimetry to all personnel. Entry dose limits set before any hot zone entry. Rotate crews to manage dose. Alpha/Beta: contamination control, full body covering. Gamma: distance is primary control — every doubling of distance reduces exposure by 75%. Radioactive material transport incidents: do NOT move shipping container — call carrier and NRC. Notify state emergency management for any radiological release.

Wildland Division

The Wildland Division operates under a separate set of standards from structural firefighting. Crew certification levels, PPE requirements, and tactical rules differ significantly. LCES is always the first priority — without it, no crew deployment.

👔
Crew typesType 1 crew (Hotshots): 20-person highly trained crews for direct/indirect attack on most dangerous terrain — 40-hour standard work week on fire, national deployment. Type 2 crew: 20-person crews for general line construction, not as high-intensity. Engine crew: Structure or wildland engines (Type 3–7) — pump-and-roll, structure protection, mop-up. Helicopter crew: Fast transport, initial attack, aerial ignition. For in-game: announce crew type when deployed.
👷
Wildland PPE (differs from structural)Nomex shirt and pants (flame-resistant, not fire-proof). Leather boots — 8-inch minimum, lug sole. Hard hat. Gloves. Goggles. Fire shelter (carried, not worn). Chainsaw operators: additional chaps, face shield. NO structural turnout gear for wildland work — it traps heat and causes heat stroke in hot weather operations. SCBA generally not used except at HazMat component of wildland fire (structures, vehicles).
📡
ICS on wildland firesLarge wildland fires use full ICS: Operations, Planning, Logistics, Finance/Admin under IC. Operations manages divisions (geographic) and groups (functional). Air Operations Branch for all aviation. Plans Section tracks fire perimeter, weather, and resources. Staging Area for incoming resources. For multi-day fires: IC transfers to Incident Management Team (IMT). All radio traffic uses ICS titles not names: "Division Alpha Supervisor, Operations."
🌬️
Weather & spot weatherRequest Spot Weather Forecast from NWS through IC for large fires — gives site-specific wind, humidity, and temperature. Monitor wind shifts constantly — unannounced wind shifts cause most burnover fatalities. Relative humidity recovery at night is critical for mop-up safety. Inversion: cooler air trapping smoke near ground — can cause sudden visibility and air quality issues for crews. Fire weather watch vs Red Flag Warning: Watch = conditions developing; Warning = conditions exist NOW.

Technical Rescue Team

Technical rescue requires specialized training and equipment beyond standard firefighting. First-due companies isolate and deny entry, establish scene safety, and request the tech rescue team. Do not attempt entry into a trench, confined space, or collapsed structure without trained personnel and proper equipment.

🏗️
Structural collapse teamCollapse zone established immediately: 1.5× building height minimum. No entry without shoring plan. Victim location: call out, listen (silence all equipment — 30-second listen periods), use search cameras, victim location device (seismic or acoustic). Mark void spaces. USAR (Urban Search and Rescue) teams for multi-story collapse. Shoring: vertical, horizontal, and cross-bracing before crew entry into collapse zone. Wood cribbing: build platforms, not just stacks.
🕳️
Confined space rescueAtmospheric test before any entry: O₂ (19.5–23.5% safe), LEL (<10% safe), CO (<25 ppm safe), H₂S (<1 ppm safe). Permit-required confined space: written entry permit, attendant at entry point, rescue procedures pre-planned. Attendant NEVER enters — call additional resources. Attendant duties: monitor entrant, maintain communication, track entry time, initiate rescue if needed. Ventilate continuously during entry if possible.
🧗
Rope rescue teamAll life safety systems: minimum 15:1 safety factor for life safety loads. Two separate anchor systems (primary + belay). Mechanical advantage systems: 3:1 (Z-rig), 4:1 (drop loop), 6:1 (piggyback). Litter rigging: patient secured with full-body contact, head-end elevated 15–30° for most rescues. Edge protection: all rope running over edges needs padding. Announce system status: "Rigging complete, two anchors, 4:1 MA, loaded — ready to lower on command."
🚗
Vehicle extricationStabilize vehicle first — cribbing, step chocks, struts for rollovers. Disable vehicle: ignition off, key removed, battery disconnect (hybrid: also manual disconnect for HV system). Glass management: side windows preferred entry — tempered glass. Windshield is laminated — cut and fold. Roof removal (total roof removal or B-post cut and fold). Dash displacement for pedal entrapment. Announce each step to medical team. Do NOT rush — patient management runs parallel with extrication.

Swift Water / Dive Team

Water rescue has four levels of response: Reach → Throw → Row → Go. Swift water certification is required before any in-water entry. Untrained entry into swift water creates two victims. The water team manages active rescue; dive team handles submerged recovery.

🌊
Swift water team operationsTeam positions: upstream lookout (spotter for approaching hazards), downstream safety (throw rope and catch swimmer), and entry team (if entering water). PFD (life jacket), helmet, wetsuit or drysuit depending on water temp, knife for entanglement, whistle. River features to ID: strainers (deadly — debris trapping), hydraulics/holes (recirculating below drops), pillows (water building against object), eddies (calm water behind obstruction — use for crew staging). Never stand in moving water above knee depth — foot entrapment.
🤿
Dive team operationsDive team activated for submerged victim, submerged vehicle, or underwater recovery. Dive supervisor manages all dive ops from surface. Tethered diving preferred in low visibility — diver connected to surface by lifeline with signal pulls: 1 pull = stop, 2 = come up, 3 = OK, 4 rapid = emergency. Never dive alone. Dive log: diver name, time in, depth, time out, bottom conditions. Cold water extends victim survival window significantly — continue rescue until confirmed. Body recovery operations are non-emergency — no rushing.
🧊
Ice rescue teamIce rescue requires drysuits, ice picks (self-rescue tools), and safety lines. Ladder on ice: horizontal approach distributes weight. Approach prone — do not stand. Victim packaging on ice: slide victim onto backboard or rescue sled, keep horizontal. Ice strength: clear blue ice = strongest (2" supports adult). White/grey/opaque = weakened, layered, or spring ice — stay off. Shore-based rescue first: throw rope, reach with ladder or pole. Never send rescuers onto ice that has already failed (victim fell through).

Search & Rescue Team (SAR)

SAR is coordinated through Incident Command with law enforcement typically taking lead on missing person cases. Fire SAR teams provide ground search, medical support, and technical rescue capability. Time is critical — most lost person survivability drops sharply after 24 hours in adverse conditions.

🐕
K9 search teamsTrailing dogs: follow specific scent trail from last known point — need uncontaminated scent article and PLS. Area search dogs: detect human scent in a defined area — best for unknown location. Cadaver dogs: locate deceased subjects. K9 team works best early — contamination from searchers degrades scent trail quickly. Keep all non-essential personnel out of PLS. Announce all K9 deployments to IC. K9 handler makes all decisions about their dog — do not give K9 handlers tactical assignments without their input.
🗺️
Probability analysisLost Person Behavior (LPB) guides search priorities by subject type: Hiker: follows trail, goes downhill, finds water. Child <6: short distance from PLS, attracted to machinery/light, hides from searchers. Dementia: path of least resistance, rarely calls out. Despondent: intentionally hidden, elevated or near water. Hunter: near vehicle, downhill, near drainage. Map probability areas before deploying teams. High probability areas get hasty teams first; systematic grid search follows.
🏥
Medical considerationsAll SAR finds assume hypothermia risk regardless of air temperature — wet + wind = rapid heat loss. Handle hypothermic victims gently — cardiac dysrhythmia from sudden movement. Do not give alcohol or rub extremities. Horizontal carry preferred. Dehydration and hypoglycemia common in prolonged missing events. Trauma from fall is always possible. Request EMS to staging early — they can be released if not needed, but cannot respond instantly if called late.

HazMat reference — PPE levels, materials, extinguishers, placard classes

EPA / OSHA Protection Levels

Level A

Highest Protection

  • Fully encapsulating, vapor-tight chemical suit
  • SCBA (worn inside suit)
  • Double chemical-resistant gloves
  • Chemical-resistant boots

Use when: highest vapor/gas/splash hazard, unknown atmosphere, IDLH confirmed

Level B

High Protection

  • Non-encapsulating chemical suit (splash-resistant, NOT vapor-tight)
  • SCBA (worn outside suit)
  • Chemical-resistant gloves (inner + outer)
  • Chemical-resistant boots

Use when: atmosphere IDLH but vapor skin hazard lower than Level A; liquid splash possible

Level C

Moderate Protection

  • Chemical-resistant suit (Tyvek or similar)
  • Air-purifying respirator (APR/PAPR) — NOT SCBA
  • Chemical-resistant gloves
  • Chemical-resistant boots

Use when: O2 adequate (>19.5%), contaminant identified, APR cartridge available, no splash expected

Level D

Minimal Protection

  • Standard work uniform / turnout gear
  • No respiratory protection beyond dust mask
  • Safety glasses, boots

Use when: atmosphere not hazardous, no splash/contact possible — cold zone only

Fire Extinguisher Classes

A

Ordinary Combustibles

Wood, paper, cloth, rubber, most plastics

Agent: Water, foam, dry chemical (ABC), CO₂

B

Flammable Liquids & Gases

Gasoline, oil, grease, paint, propane, acetylene

Agent: Foam, dry chemical (ABC/BC), CO₂, Halon

C

Energized Electrical

Wiring, panels, motors, appliances — live circuits

Agent: CO₂, dry chemical (ABC/BC), Halon — NEVER water or foam

D

Combustible Metals

Magnesium, sodium, titanium, lithium, potassium

Agent: Dry powder (Metal-X, Lith-X) ONLY — water causes violent reaction

K

Cooking Oils & Fats

Deep fryers, cooking oils, animal/vegetable fats

Agent: Wet chemical only. Do NOT use water — causes grease explosion

DOT HazMat Placard Classes

Class 1Explosives — Divisions 1.1 (mass explosion) through 1.6 (extremely insensitive). Orange placard with explosion symbol.
Class 2Gases — 2.1 Flammable gas (propane/hydrogen), 2.2 Non-flammable compressed gas, 2.3 Toxic/poison gas. Stage upwind and uphill.
Class 3Flammable Liquids — Gasoline, diesel, ethanol, acetone. Red diamond. Flashpoint below 140°F (60°C).
Class 4Flammable Solids — 4.1 Flammable solid, 4.2 Spontaneously combustible, 4.3 Dangerous when wet (reacts with water). DO NOT use water on Class 4.3.
Class 5Oxidizers & Organic Peroxides — 5.1 Oxidizer (increases fire intensity), 5.2 Organic peroxide (heat/shock sensitive). Yellow placard.
Class 6Toxic / Infectious — 6.1 Poison/toxic material (pesticides, cyanide), 6.2 Infectious substance (biological). Full Level A consideration.
Class 7Radioactive — White-I (surface <0.5 mR/hr), Yellow-II, Yellow-III. Contact radiation control officer immediately. Time/Distance/Shielding.
Class 8Corrosives — Acids (sulfuric, hydrochloric) and bases (sodium hydroxide). Black and white placard. Causes tissue destruction on contact.
Class 9Miscellaneous Dangerous Goods — Dry ice (CO₂), lithium batteries, environmentally hazardous substances, elevated temperature materials.

Common HazMat Materials — quick reference

Ammonia (NH₃) — Class 2.3

  • Hazard: Toxic gas, flammable (15–28% in air), corrosive
  • Odor: Pungent — detectable at 5 ppm (IDLH = 300 ppm)
  • PPE: Level A for spill/leak; Level B if concentration known low
  • Action: Evacuate downwind, fog water spray to knock vapors (do not flood)
  • Common source: Refrigeration systems, fertilizer

Chlorine (Cl₂) — Class 2.3

  • Hazard: Highly toxic gas, heavier than air (pools in low areas), corrosive
  • Odor: Sharp bleach-like (IDLH = 10 ppm)
  • PPE: Level A — no exceptions for confirmed chlorine
  • Action: Large isolation distance, downwind evacuation; water fog reduces vapor cloud
  • Common source: Water treatment, pool chemicals, tanker trucks

Propane / LPG — Class 2.1

  • Hazard: Flammable gas (2.1–9.5% LEL), heavier than air, BLEVE risk
  • PPE: Full structural PPE + SCBA; no sparks or flame sources
  • Action: Shut supply if safe, cool tank with water (do NOT extinguish leak without stopping flow), evacuate 1000 ft if BLEVE risk
  • Common source: Tanks, cylinders, vehicles, homes

Gasoline / Diesel — Class 3

  • Hazard: Flammable liquid, vapors heavier than air, long ignition range
  • PPE: Structural PPE + SCBA
  • Action: Eliminate ignition sources, foam suppression for fires, absorbent for spills; prevent runoff to drains
  • Common source: Tanker trucks, underground storage tanks, vehicles

Sodium / Potassium — Class 4.3

  • Hazard: Reacts violently with water — produces hydrogen gas and intense heat
  • PPE: Level A for spill
  • Action: NEVER apply water. Use dry sand, Class D extinguisher, or smother. Isolate from all moisture sources.
  • Common source: Chemical plants, laboratories

Sulfuric Acid — Class 8

  • Hazard: Highly corrosive, exothermic reaction with water, releases toxic SO₂ vapor
  • PPE: Level A for concentrated acid; Level B/C for dilute
  • Action: Dilute with large volumes of water (add water to area, not acid to water); contain runoff; neutralize with baking soda if needed
  • Common source: Batteries, industrial processes, tanker trucks

Carbon Monoxide (CO) — Class 2.3

  • Hazard: Colorless, odorless, highly toxic (IDLH = 1,200 ppm), flammable
  • PPE: SCBA mandatory — CO passes through APR cartridges
  • Action: Ventilate immediately, remove all occupants, monitor atmosphere before re-entry
  • Common source: Vehicle exhaust, generators, heating systems, structure fires

Lithium Batteries — Class 9

  • Hazard: Thermal runaway — fire may re-ignite hours/days after knockdown; toxic smoke (HF gas from EV batteries)
  • PPE: SCBA, structural PPE
  • Action: Massive water volume for suppression; do not attempt to move burning EV; isolate and monitor for re-ignition; notify disposal unit
  • Common source: Electric vehicles, e-bikes, phones, power tools, energy storage

Incident Severity Levels — Fire Ground

Level 1 Routine

  • Controlled with on-scene resources
  • Single or double alarm
  • No special teams needed
  • Examples: car fire, dumpster fire, minor structure fire, small HazMat spill

Level 2 Elevated

  • Additional resources required — mutual aid requested
  • Multiple alarms
  • Technical teams may be needed (HazMat, rescue)
  • Examples: working structure fire, multi-vehicle accident with entrapment, significant HazMat release

Level 3 Major Incident

  • Regional / state resources required
  • Full ICS activation
  • EOC may be activated
  • Examples: large commercial fire, Mayday with entrapment, mass casualty HazMat, wildland-urban interface fire

Fire Behavior

FlashoverAll combustible surfaces in a room ignite simultaneously — room temperature reaches 1,100°F+. Immediate warning: rollover (flames rolling at ceiling).
BackdraftSuperheated oxygen-deprived fire erupts when fresh air introduced. Warning signs: puffing smoke, black oily smoke, blistering paint, heat without flames.
RolloverUnburned gases at ceiling ignite in rolling flames — precursor to flashover. Get low; advance attack line immediately.
LCESLookout / Communications / Escape Routes / Safety Zones — wildland fire safety system; establish before any commitment
Fire TriangleFuel + Heat + Oxygen — remove any one to extinguish. Water = removes heat. Foam = removes O₂ and fuel contact. CO₂ = removes O₂.
Fire TetrahedronFuel + Heat + Oxygen + Chain Reaction — Halon/dry chemical interrupt the chain reaction to extinguish
BTUBritish Thermal Unit — unit of heat energy. Fire intensity measured in BTU/hr.

Radio communications — fire ground

Radio format — NIMS standard

  • Who you're calling first, then your unit: "Engine 2, Engine 1."
  • Wait for acknowledgment before transmitting message
  • Keep transmissions brief — no unnecessary words
  • Repeat critical info back: "Copy, proceeding to Division 2 Charlie."
  • Announce tactical benchmarks unprompted — don't wait for IC to ask

Mayday transmission

  • Transmit: "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY" three times
  • State: Unit / Name → Location → What happened → Air supply → Resources needed
  • Activate PASS device
  • Move to safe area if possible — walls, corners
  • Conserve air — reduce movement
  • IC clears radio: "All units — Mayday declared. Tactical channel 2 for all others."
